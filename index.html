Biological Factors in Library Materials

Protecting collections from living threats and slow deterioration.

Biological factors are living organisms that feed on or colonize library and archival materials. Insects, rodents, fungi, and mold can weaken paper, stain surfaces, and damage bindings. Preservation reduces loss, ensures access, and safeguards cultural memory for future readers.

Explore Threats Preservation Practices

Why Preservation Matters

  • Stabilize temperature and humidity to slow decay.
  • Reduce pests with monitoring and safe storage.
  • Maintain access to rare and fragile collections.
  • Document risks before damage spreads.

Types of Biological Threats

Biological damage often begins quietly. The organisms below target cellulose, adhesives, leather, and textile fibers, leaving tunnels, stains, and brittleness across collections.

IN

MICRO-ORGANISM:

Fungi: The Silent Destoryers

Grow in high humidity, warmth and poor air circulation or ventilation.

Bacteria: The Undetectable Menace

Thrive on damp surfaces and dirty hands

FG

INSECTS

Silverfish

Small, silvery insect attracted to starches and sugars found in papers.

Cockroasches

Feed on the organic materials found in books, including the adhesives used in binding and the paper itself.

Book Lice

Tiny isects that thrive in damp environments and are considered a biological threat to library materials because they feed on mold and binding components.

Termites and Bookworms

Termites: Attack wood, shelves, and large amount of paper. Bookworms: Focus mainly on individuals books.

BA

Bacteria

Microbial activity accelerates chemical breakdown of paper, photographs, and adhesives.

Causes of Infestation

Biological damage is often triggered by environmental imbalance. Stable climate control and good housekeeping minimize risk across collections.

Humidity and Temperature

High humidity and warm temperatures create ideal growth conditions for mold and increase insect activity.

Poor Ventilation

Stagnant air traps moisture. Without airflow, spores and pests spread more quickly between shelves.

Improper Storage

Overcrowded shelves, cardboard debris, and organic packaging provide food and shelter for pests.

Infrequent Cleaning

Dust and residue support microbial growth. Regular cleaning reduces nutrients and early infestations.

Effects on Library Materials

Biological factors affect every layer of a book or record. Damage is often irreversible once fibers are weakened.

Paper and Documents

Insects and mold degrade cellulose, leaving holes, stains, and brittle pages.

Leather and Cloth

Bindings and covers dry out, split, or develop fuzzy mold growth that weakens structure.

Adhesives

Glues and pastes become soft targets for pests and can fail under microbial activity.

Photographs and Media

Emulsions and film layers are sensitive to moisture, leading to discoloration and loss of detail.

Prevention and Control

Preservation strategies focus on prevention first. Libraries use combined environmental, procedural, and monitoring practices to protect collections.

CL

Climate Control

Maintain steady temperature and humidity levels to slow biological activity.

ST

Safe Storage

Use archival boxes, spacers, and breathable shelving to minimize crowding.

MN

Monitoring

Deploy traps and inspection schedules to detect pests early.

CL

Cleaning Routines

Regular dusting and surface cleaning reduce nutrients and spore accumulation.
